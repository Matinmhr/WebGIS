Processing Workflow

This document describes the processing pipeline followed to produce all project outputs. We work with 3 types of pollutant (NO₂, PM₂.₅, PM₁₀). We applied the same methodological workflow independently on each pollutant, over the same study area (Belgium) and the same reference period (2021–2023). All spatial operations were performed in QGIS using the WGS 84 coordinate reference system (EPSG:4326).

01 / 08

Pollutant maps for December 2023

The starting point of the analysis required producing monthly-average raster maps of NO₂, PM₂.₅, and PM₁₀ concentration for December 2023, clipped to the national boundaries of the case study.

Data acquisition

Hourly pollutant concentration data were downloaded from the CAMS European Air Quality Reanalysis platform (Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service) in NetCDF format. The download configuration used the Ensemble median model, surface level (0), Validated Reanalysis type, for December 2023, covering NO₂, PM₂.₅, and PM₁₀ simultaneously.

02 / 08

Annual average pollutant maps for 2023

To characterise the general air quality conditions across the study area, the 12 monthly rasters for each pollutant were aggregated into a single annual mean for both 2021 and 2023. We compute also the 2021 averages as they are needed for the temporal trend analysis in Step 4.

Monthly data and clipping

For the year 2021, monthly GeoTIFF files were pre-processed and available in the shared project folder. For 2023, the monthly maps produced in Step 1 were supplemented by the remaining 11 months available in the same repository. All 12 monthly rasters per year were clipped to the national boundary before aggregation, ensuring CRS consistency across all inputs.

03 / 08

Concentration classification maps for 2023

The annual mean rasters were reclassified into five discrete concentration levels according to the EU annual limit values established by the European Environment Agency (EEA), enabling a direct assessment of regulatory compliance and spatial distribution of exposure risk.

04 / 08

Annual Mean Aggregation Change 2021–2023

Before integrating land cover data, a bitemporal difference map was produced for each pollutant to independently quantify the spatial evolution of air quality concentrations over the 2021–2023 period. This change detection layer identifies areas of improvement, deterioration, or stability.

Bitemporal difference

The AMAC (Annual Mean Aggregation Change) raster was derived by subtracting the 2021 annual mean from the 2023 annual mean using the Raster Calculator. Positive values indicate areas where concentrations increased (environmental risk zones); negative values indicate areas of improvement; values near zero represent atmospheric stability. The two input rasters were verified to share the same spatial extent and resolution prior to computation.

05 / 08

Land Cover Change map 2021–2023

To contextualise air quality trends within land use dynamics, a land cover change detection map was produced using the ESRI 10m Annual Land Cover product (ArcGIS Living Atlas of the World) for 2021 and 2023.

Land cover preprocessing

GeoTIFF tiles for 2021 and 2023 were downloaded, reprojected to WGS 84, merged where the study area spanned multiple tiles, and clipped to the national boundary. A pixel value of 0 was assigned as NoData. Symbology was applied using the standard style file provided in the course repository.

Cloud pixels (code 10) are treated as NoData throughout the entire transition analysis. Any transition involving code 10 — either as origin (10xx) or destination (x10) — is excluded from statistical computations.
06 / 08

Zonal statistics: land cover transitions vs. pollutant change

To spatially correlate land cover transitions with changes in pollutant concentrations, zonal statistics (mean, minimum, maximum) of the AMAC raster were extracted for three land cover change zones — Stable, Gain, and Loss — corresponding to the classes.

Binary change mask and resampling

Because CAMS data (~1 km/pixel) and ESRI Land Cover (~10 m/pixel) operate at very different spatial resolutions, the AMAC raster was resampled to match the land cover grid resolution before any spatial overlap analysis. A binary change mask was then produced from the LCC raster using the GDAL Raster Calculator, classifying pixels into three categories: Stable (land cover unchanged), Gain (new areas of the target class in 2023), and Loss (areas that were the target class in 2021 but changed by 2023). Cloud transitions were excluded by design through the mask formula.

07 / 08

Bivariate map: pollutant concentration and population density

A bivariate choropleth map at GAUL Administrative Level 2 was produced to visualise the joint spatial distribution of pollutant concentration and population density, identifying areas simultaneously exposed to high pollution and high population counts.

Population data and classification

Population counts for 2023 were sourced from WorldPop (Unconstrained, 100 m resolution). Internal NoData values (-99999) within the study area were reset to zero before classification, while preserving NoData outside the boundary. Five population classes were derived using quantile-based boundaries computed with r.quantile (4 quantiles + class 1 for zero-population cells). The population raster was then reclassified accordingly.

08 / 08

Population exposure by concentration class — pie chart

The final output quantifies the share of the national population exposed to each of the five EU concentration classes, providing a synthetic indicator of public health exposure that integrates both spatial extent and population density.

Aggregation and population sum per class

The bivariate layer from Step 7 was dissolved by the pol_class_max field, producing at most five features — one per concentration level. Against this dissolved layer, a new zonal statistics run was performed using the original (non-classified) population count raster, computing the total population sum within each concentration zone. The result is a compact vector layer with up to five rows associating each pollution class with the total population exposed to it.
